Transit in Beijing

Analyzing transportation behavior in Beijing from 2007 to 2012 to identify trends.

Background

In major cities across the world, understanding transportation behaviors is a critical task for city planning and investments. This project will visualize key spatial and temporal trends associated with transportation, specifically recorded activities within the 5th Ring Road of Beijing. Beijing is one of the most densely populated cities in the world, and is host to a sophisticated and robust transportation network. While the focus of this project will be on Beijing, insights gained will likely apply generally to other cities.

Data Source

This study used the Geolife data produced by Microsoft Research Asia. This GPS trajectory dataset included data for 182 users over a period of over five years (2007 to 2012). The data includes over 17,621 different trajectories or segments recorded by different GPS loggers and phones with a variety of sampling rates. The dataset demonstrates various types of outdoor activities to include foot traffic, traveling via cars, taking public transportation, cycling, etc. Not all trajectories are provided a label, but our project included those that have been classified with a specific transportation type.

Target Audience

City planners, transportation engineers, and policy makers will benefit from the insights gained from this project. Users will be able to see, by type of transportation, changes in travel behaviors by time of day, day of the week, and month of the year. For example, do drivers begin their commute earlier in the day than those that walk, given most walkers begin their transit from a location much closer to their destination as opposed to drivers. This will support insights into areas that will likely require more frequent maintenance and/or priority resolutions to issues to limit interruptions to daily transportation behaviors.

Analysis & Hypotheses

Temporal

Hypothesis: Transportation activities in both duration and quantity change notably based on time, with the majority of activity concentrated in the summer months and with the longest commutes ending during end-of-day hours.

Spatial

Hypothesis: Commuting channels and most common transit methods will be denser and more varied in the inner city, and around national holidays.

Composition

Conclusions

In conclusion, it's clear that time, location, and mode of transportation have notable impacts on transportation activities in Beijing's 5th Ring Road.

More specifically, the amount of concurrent travelers and the longest duration of travel peaks in May and then in September and October. This aligns to changing weather and issues with pollution during the summer months. Similarly, mid-morning around 0900 and in the evening between 1900 and 2100 have the greatest amount of concurrent travelers, aligning with conventional work hours. Somewhat surprisingly, there was more travelon the weekends, namely Friday and Saturday, as compared to the work week, signaling an issue with weekend construction.

When looking at trends geographically, the yearly pattern of travel spikes in the late summer and early fall aligns with Chinese national public holidays. The two largest holidays of the year are the Mid-Autumn Festival (September) and National Day (October). Chinese employers typically provide less paid time off than American employers, but public holidays are typically paid time off from work and sometimes last for one to two weeks. Many Chinese take this opportunity to travel and visit family. Before these long periods of time off, many companies will work extended hours -- late nights and through the weekends -- in the weeks leading up to the holiday. Geographically, it's also easy to see where modes of transportation are more or less common. Bus routes cover the city and are well-utilized by citizens. Bicycles are also well-used, as well as walking, moreso in Beijing's University district and near the Olympic Stadium and Beijing's largest inner-city public park. Subways are well-used in the city, and trains are more used on the outskirts. Cars and taxis are also well-used.

With composition, the percentage of travel records associated with walking is generally the highest with the exception of July and August, where bus travel recordings are greatest, and January and February, when biking is highest followed by driving. As with activity levels, the change in proportion of bus usage aligns with high temperatires and pollution. The use of bikes in the winter months is slightly surprising, but the absolute value of travel is extremely limited. Walkers tend to make up the greatest percentage of travel on the weekends, with bus and bike riders accounting for most of the transportation during the weekdays. Finally, walkers and bikers make up the greatest percentage of travelers during the work hours, with most travelers riding the bus in the early morning prior to the start of the workday. That said, any construction or upgrades would still be most ideal between 1 and 6 in the morning, as the absolute activity is extremely low across all transportation types.
